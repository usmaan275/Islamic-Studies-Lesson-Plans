# Year 9 Islamic Studies — Lesson Plan  
**Topic:** From the Umayyads to the Modern Muslim World  
**Date:** Friday 16th January 2026  
**Time:** 5:00 – 6:10 PM (70 mins)  

---

## 5:00 – 5:10 (10 mins) | Starter Activity (Late Arrivals Expected)

### Starter Question:
- What do you already know about early Muslim empires after ʿAlī رضي الله عنه?

**Teacher note:**  
- Allow students **5 minutes** to discuss in pairs or small groups (4–5 students).  
- Collect a few responses to gauge prior knowledge and connect to today’s content.

**Reflection / Quote:**  
> *“Indeed, Allah is over all things competent.”* (Ṣūrah al-Baqarah 2:20)  
> Even when knowledge seems limited, Allah’s plan unfolds perfectly in history.

---

## 5:10 – 5:30 (20 mins) | The Umayyad Empire & Early Expansion  

### Key Points:
- **Umayyad Caliphate (661–750 CE)**  
  - First major dynasty after Rashidūn Khulafāʾ  
  - Capital: Damascus  
  - Focused on **administration, taxation, and expansion**
- **Expansion to South Asia**:  
  - Muhammad ibn Qāsim رحمه الله conquered Sindh (now Pakistan) in 711 CE  
  - Spread Islam peacefully and established governance structures  
  - Students relate this to their heritage  

### Discussion Time 1 (5 mins)
> Why was early administration important for the stability of the Muslim empire?  

**Teacher guidance:** trade, justice, collecting taxes, uniting diverse regions

**Reflection / Quote:**  
> *“Allah changes not the condition of a people until they change what is in themselves.”* (Ṣūrah ar-Raʿd 13:11)  
> Even small acts of governance, courage, and justice can shape entire regions.

---

## 5:30 – 5:40 (10 mins) | Andalusia & the Abbasids  

### Key Points:
- **Al-Andalus (Spain, 711–1492)**  
  - Cultural and scientific achievements  
  - Famous centers: Córdoba, Granada  
- **Abbasid Caliphate (750–1258)**  
  - Capital: Baghdad  
  - Golden Age: mathematics, medicine, astronomy, philosophy  
  - Students may know **al-Khwārizmī** (algebra) and **Ibn Sīnā** (medicine)

### Discussion Time 2 (3 mins)
> How does knowledge and science strengthen a civilisation?  

**Teacher guidance:** trade, technology, learning, reputation

**Reflection / Quote:**  
> *“He grants wisdom to whom He wills.”* (Ṣūrah al-Baqarah 2:269)  
> The Golden Age shows Allah granting wisdom to guide and uplift entire communities.

---

## 5:40 – 5:50 (10 mins) | Ayyubids, Crusades & Mamluks  

### Key Points:
- **Ayyubid dynasty (Saladin)**  
  - Defended Jerusalem from Crusaders  
  - United Muslim factions for justice and defense  
- **Crusades**  
  - European attempts to capture holy lands  
  - Centuries of conflict ensued  
- **Mamluks (Egypt, 1250–1517)**  
  - Slave soldiers became rulers  
  - Defeated Mongols at Ain Jalut (1260 CE)  
  - Protected Islamic lands from destruction

### Discussion Time 3 (3 mins)
> Why was the Mamluk victory against the Mongols so important?  

**Teacher guidance:** preserved Islamic culture, prevented mass destruction, safeguarded knowledge

**Reflection / Quote:**  
> *“Indeed, Allah is the best of planners.”* (Ṣūrah Āl ʿImrān 3:54)  
> Even overwhelming invaders can be turned back by Allah’s will, showing His control over history.

---

## 5:50 – 6:00 (10 mins) | Mongol Invasion & Ottoman Empire  

### Key Points:
- **Mongol Invasion (1258 CE)**  
  - Sacked Baghdad, ending Abbasid political power  
  - Many scholars and libraries destroyed  
  - Eventually, many Mongols accepted Islam, showing Allah’s ability to change hearts  
- **Ottoman Empire (1299–1924)**  
  - Capital: Constantinople/Istanbul  
  - Re-established Islamic unity  
  - Famous sultans: Mehmed II, Suleiman the Magnificent  
  - Influence spread into South Asia and set precedents for law and governance

### Discussion Time 4 (3 mins)
> How can a strong empire unite such diverse peoples?  

**Teacher guidance:** law, religion, centralised administration, trade networks

**Reflection / Quote:**  
> *“And if Allah had willed, He could have made you one people.”* (Ṣūrah al-Hujurāt 49:13)  
> Diversity requires careful leadership and justice; Allah tests how humans manage unity.

---

## 6:00 – 6:05 (5 mins) | Mughal Empire in South Asia  

### Key Points:
- **Mughal Empire (1526–1857)**  
  - Founded by Babur in northern India  
  - Famous rulers: Akbar (tolerance & administration), Shah Jahan (Taj Mahal)  
  - Contributions: architecture, art, governance, religious culture  
  - Students can relate this directly to Pakistan/Bangladesh heritage  

### Discussion Time 5 (2 mins)
> How did the Mughals leave a lasting legacy on South Asia?  

**Teacher guidance:** law, administration, trade, culture, monuments, Islamic influence

**Reflection / Quote:**  
> *“So which of the favors of your Lord would you deny?”* (Ṣūrah ar-Rahmān 55:13)  
> The richness of culture, learning, and governance shows the blessings of Allah in these lands.

---

## 6:05 – 6:10 (5 mins) | Transition to Modern Muslim World  

### Key Points:
- Decline of empires due to internal strife, European colonialism, and wars  
- **South Asia after Mughal decline:**  
  - British colonised India (1757 onwards)  
  - Many Muslims lost political power but kept religious and cultural identity  
  - Partition in 1947 created **Pakistan** as a homeland for Muslims  
  - Bangladesh gained independence in 1971  
- Modern Muslim world: continuation of Islamic scholarship, cultural identity, and resilience

### Final Reflection / Quote:
> *“Allah changes the condition of a people only after they change what is in themselves.”* (Ṣūrah ar-Raʿd 13:11)  
> Even through hardship and decline, revival comes through faith, knowledge, and unity.

---

### End of Lesson

Today’s lesson is about **belief in the existence of God**, not just emotionally — but **intellectually**.

Muslims do not believe blindly.

We believe using:
- **Our senses**
- **Our logic**
- **Revelation (Waḥy)**

And when all three are understood properly — belief becomes strong.

# 5:05–5:15 | The Three Ways Humans Know Things

Write on board:

**1 — The Five Senses**  
**2 — Logic**  
**3 — Revelation (Waḥy)**

---

## The Five Senses — Powerful but Limited

Ask students:

How do we know something exists?

We:
- See
- Hear
- Touch
- Smell
- Taste

These are powerful tools.

But they only work **up to a point**.

---

### Example — The Wind

Ask:

Can you see the wind?

No.

But can you see its effects?

Yes:
- Trees moving
- Leaves shaking
- Dust blowing

So even though you **cannot see the wind**, you know it exists because of its **effects**.

Likewise:
We may not see God directly —
but we see **His effects everywhere**.

---

## Logic — Also Powerful but Limited

Explain:

Logic helps us understand things we cannot directly sense.

Example:

If you walk into a classroom and see:
- Chairs moved
- Bags open
- Whiteboard written on

Even if nobody is there,
you know someone was there before.

Because:
**effects require causes**.

---

## Waḥy — When Logic Stops

Explain carefully:

There are some things:
- We cannot see
- We cannot test
- We cannot fully understand

That is where **Waḥy** (revelation) comes in.

Example:

We believe in:
- Angels
- The Day of Judgement
- Paradise
- Hellfire

We cannot see these now.

But Allah told us about them.

And the One who created everything knows what lies beyond our senses.

This is called:
**ʿIlm al-Ghayb — knowledge of the unseen.**

# 5:50–6:05 | Arguments for the Existence of God

Now we reinforce the key arguments.

Speak clearly and slowly — this is the **core of the lesson**.

---

# Argument 1 — Surroundings

Ask:

Look around this room.

Everything you see:
- Walls
- Lights
- Tables
- Phones

Did they appear by themselves?

No.

They were designed.
They were built.
They were placed.

So ask:

If a classroom needs a builder…
how much more does the **universe** need one?

---

# Argument 2 — Creation

Look at human beings.

- Eyes that can detect light
- Ears that detect sound
- A heart beating without stopping
- Skin that repairs itself

Ask:

Did this happen randomly?

Or was it designed?

Even a simple machine needs planning.

The human body is **millions of times more complex**.

Design points to **a Designer**.

---

# Argument 3 — Cause and Effect

Everything that begins must have a cause.

A phone has a maker.
A building has a builder.

So ask:

The universe began.
Science itself says it had a beginning.

So what caused it?

Something outside it.
Something powerful.
Something timeless.

That is God.

---

# Argument 4 — Patterns in the Universe

This is where you give strong examples.

Explain:

Look at patterns in nature:

- The sun rises at precise times
- The moon follows cycles
- Seasons repeat
- Human DNA follows structured codes

Nothing behaves randomly.

Everything follows **laws**.

---

## Crazy Pattern Examples

Mention examples students will remember:

- Bees build perfect hexagons — shapes mathematicians recognise as efficient
- Snowflakes form intricate symmetrical designs
- Planets orbit in exact predictable paths
- Human fingerprints — billions of people, no duplicates

Ask:

Random things do not produce perfect patterns repeatedly.

Patterns suggest **order**.

Order suggests **intelligence**.

---

# Argument 5 — “Crazy Coincidences”

Give striking examples.

Explain:

Some people say everything happened by coincidence.

But think carefully.

Imagine:

You throw a handful of letters into the air…
and they land forming a full paragraph.

Possible?

Technically yes.

Likely?

Almost impossible.

Now imagine:
That happening again.
And again.
And again.

At some point,
you stop calling it coincidence —
and start calling it **design**.

The universe is not one coincidence.

It is **millions of precise events happening perfectly**.

That cannot happen randomly.

## Key Contributions

Muslim scholars developed:

- Logical reasoning about existence
- Arguments about cause and creation
- Ways to defend belief against atheism
- Systems for debating respectfully

Fields included:

- Kalām (Islamic theology)
- Philosophy
- Logic
- Mathematics

They showed that:
**Faith and intellect go together.**

Say slowly:

but you believe it exists.

You cannot see gravity —
but you believe it exists.

You cannot see the wind —
but you believe it exists.

So when we see:
- Order
- Design
- Patterns
- Causes
